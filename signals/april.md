## Apr 1–15, 2026

- The dominant driver over these two weeks was still the U.S.-Iran situation, 
but what’s more interesting is how quickly equities started trading *past* it. 
By mid-April, the S&P was back at record highs on de-escalation chatter. That 
feels less like fundamentals improving and more like the market deciding the 
tail risk won’t materialize.

- There’s a pretty clear divergence underneath the surface. Housing is weakening
meaningfully — builder sentiment down, existing home sales soft — which makes 
sense given higher yields and elevated input costs. So while the index looks 
strong, rate-sensitive parts of the economy are already feeling the damage.

- Inflation doesn’t feel “resolved” here. Import prices picked up in March, and 
oil had a much bigger move after that. It seems likely the real inflation impact 
of the energy shock just hasn’t shown up in the data yet. The market is probably 
front-running a clean normalization that hasn’t actually happened.

- The Fed’s stance is basically: do nothing and wait. No urgency to cut, but 
also no tightening reaction. What stands out is that equities are rallying 
*without* any policy support narrative — that usually makes the move feel a 
bit more fragile.

- Cross-asset signals are more cautious than equities. The dollar has given back 
its war premium and markets are leaning toward containment, but bonds and housing 
aren’t really confirming a clean recovery. It feels like different parts of the 
market are pricing different versions of the same story.

- There’s also a background risk that feels under-discussed: the increasing 
political pressure on the Fed. It’s not moving markets yet, but if it escalates, 
it could turn into a credibility issue rather than just a policy-path question.

**Two-week read:**  
Feels like the market has shifted from pricing the shock to pricing the 
*resolution*, probably a bit too quickly. Equities are behaving as if the 
worst-case scenario is off the table, while rates and real-economy data suggest 
the after-effects are still working through the system. 

**Open question:**  
If rising oil prices start pushing inflation higher, will the market stay 
optimistic because things seem to be improving, or will it shift back to 
worrying that interest rates will stay high and hold stocks back?
