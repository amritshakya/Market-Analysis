## Moat

### What it is
A company's durable competitive advantage that allows it to maintain profits and defend its market position over time.

### Why it matters
- Determines whether a company can sustain high returns on capital
- Helps differentiate between short-term winners and long-term compounders
- Strong moats reduce competition and pricing pressure

### Types of moats
- Network effects (e.g., platforms becoming more valuable as users increase)
- Switching costs (hard for customers to leave)
- Brand power (pricing ability, customer loyalty)
- Cost advantage (lower production or operating costs)
- Intangible assets (patents, proprietary technology)

### How it shows up in investing
- Consistently high margins or returns on capital
- Ability to raise prices without losing customers
- Stable or growing market share over time

### Current relevance (2026)
With AI disrupting parts of software, companies with weaker moats are more vulnerable. The market is increasingly rewarding firms with durable advantages (e.g., infrastructure players) while questioning those with more fragile business models.

### My takeaway
Not all growth is equal — companies with strong moats can compound over time, while those without are more exposed to disruption.
